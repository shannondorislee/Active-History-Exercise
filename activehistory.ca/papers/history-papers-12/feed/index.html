Subject: Recognition of American Vietnam War era draftees

Prime Minister Harper:

As Prime Minster, you must understand the inherent obligations citizens have to their country. During the United States involvement in the Vietnam War, Canada offered sanctuary to nearly 50,000 United States men, of military draft age, who refused to honor their moral and legal obligation to their country. Our President, Jimmy Carter, pardoned those men on January 21, 1977 for “…violations of the Military Selective Service Act…” if their actions did not “… involve force or violence”. Many returned to their former country after the pardon.

In my opinion, the American men who fled to your country, to avoid the Vietnam War era draft, should have applied for conscientious objector status. However I suspect that few would have qualified. We draftees did what our country expected and needed during a time of war but we have not received any recognition for that service to America. However draft dodgers continue to make the news: It was reported that Castlegar, B.C, in July of 2006, hosted a “Vietnam War Draft-Dodgers Reunion”. Also at least one United States veteran’s group view Vietnam War draft dodgers as anti-war heroes so draftees have been forgotten.

Vietnam War draftees, which include men who filled draft dodgers slots, had the moral courage, not shown by the draft dodgers, to serve their country. So I ask you to help in getting some type of formal recognition for the American men who did no need to be pardoned nor did they expect Canada to protect them from their government.

Your laws made it possible for young American men to avoid their moral and legal obligations to their country. So it is seems reasonable to ask you for help in honoring the American men who did not use the Canadian government as an aide. My recommendation for an American Conscription Ribbon is enclosed.

Sincerely,

William Anthony Cook, American Vietnam War Draftee 1965 – Vietnam 1966/67
